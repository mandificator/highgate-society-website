The Reality

Every generation faces a disruption that rewrites the rules. Industrialisation. The internet. This is yours.

The pattern is always the same: a technology emerges that changes what work means, who gets rewarded, and how value is created. The professionals who thrive aren't the ones who predict the future — they're the ones who understand themselves clearly enough to adapt.

Right now, that disruption is AI. Parts of your work — analysis, writing, research, first drafts — are already done faster and cheaper by machines. That's not a forecast. It's a fact.

The question isn't whether change affects you. It always does. The question is: what do you do with the parts that are still yours? What becomes more valuable now? What do you let go of?

Most professionals are stuck between two bad responses: ignoring the shift entirely, or chasing every new tool without a strategy. There's a third option.

Our Approach

We don't teach you how to use AI. We help you decide what to do about it.

This isn't a course on tools. It's not a technology workshop.

It's a structured process for thinking clearly about what changes — and what doesn't — when machines can do the repeatable parts of your work. Your strategy, your leadership, your positioning, your next move.

The disruption is technological. The response is personal. It depends on your strengths, your values, the business you're building, and the way you lead.

We start there.

The Framework

Built on 50 years of research.
Applied to today's disruption.

Our methodology integrates three of the most validated frameworks
in career science and change management, developed at MIT, Ohio State, and across 13 countries.

01

Career Adapt-Abilities

Mark L. Savickas

Ohio State · Northeastern Ohio Universities

Four psychological resources that determine how well someone navigates disruption: Concern, Control, Curiosity, Confidence. Validated across 13 countries. Over 1,000 peer-reviewed studies.

How we use it: we measure your adaptability profile — where you're strong and where you're stuck.

02

The Transition Model

William Bridges

Author of Managing Transitions (1991)

Change is external — it happens to you. Transition is internal — it's how you process it. Three phases: Ending, Neutral Zone, New Beginning. Most people try to skip the middle. The middle is where the real thinking happens.

How we use it: we identify where you are psychologically — not just practically.

03

Career Anchors

Edgar H. Schein

MIT Sloan School of Management

Eight career motivators that stay stable across a lifetime. Your anchor is what you won't sacrifice — autonomy, expertise, impact, security, the drive to build.

How we use it: we understand what drives you — so the path forward is yours, not a template.

Your adaptability profile. Your relationship with AI right now — what you're using, what you're avoiding, what you haven't thought about yet. The group meets, the ground rules are set.

Weeks 2–4 — The hard questions

Each week, one or two members bring a real situation to the group. Not abstract strategy — the actual decisions you're facing. The group works through it with you — different industries, different blind spots, better answers.

Weeks 5–6 — Action & accountability

You've done the thinking. Now you execute. Shorter sessions. What did you do? What happened? Where are you stuck? The group holds you to it.
